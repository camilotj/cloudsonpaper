I came to painting late. Not as a practitioner — I've never been able to draw — but as a student of seeing. It started with a single afternoon in front of a Bonnard at the Tate, standing there for longer than I'd planned, trying to understand why the light in that room felt more real than the light outside.

The answer had something to do with temperature. With the way Bonnard let colours bleed into each other without apologising. And it changed, slowly and permanently, how I work with a camera.

## Colour is a relationship, not a property

The first thing painting teaches you is that colour doesn't exist independently. A red next to a green is not the same red as a red next to a grey. The eye is always comparing, always reading context. Painters know this instinctively. Photographers, trained on single images in isolation, often forget it.

When I shoot, I now think less about the colour of the subject and more about the colour conversation happening in the frame. What is the shadow doing to the wall beside it? What does the warmth of the lamp say about the coolness of the window? These are painter's questions. They make better photographs.

## The Impressionists and the overcast day

I used to prefer shooting in golden hour like everyone else. Then I spent time looking at how the Impressionists handled overcast light — and I started to understand what I'd been missing.

Flat light doesn't flatten. It reveals. Without harsh shadows to create drama, you're forced to find structure in colour, in texture, in the subtlety of tone. Monet's haystacks in grey November light are more complex than his haystacks in summer. The difficulty made him look harder.

I brought this to my own work. Some of my most considered photographs were made on days when other photographers put their cameras away.

## Distance changes everything

Cézanne understood something that took me years to find in my own images: that distance is not just spatial — it's chromatic. Things that are far away are not just smaller. They are cooler, bluer, lighter. The atmosphere between you and the mountain changes the mountain.

In photography we call this aerial perspective, but we rarely use it deliberately. Painters built entire systems around it. When I started thinking about distance in terms of colour temperature rather than focal length, my images became quieter and more accurate — more honest about what it actually feels like to be in a landscape.

## What I carry from the studio to the street

I don't paint. But I carry a painter's habits into every job. I look at the edge of things. I look at what colours are adjacent. I ask what the light is doing that is unexpected, specific, unrepeatable.

A painter has the luxury of time and the penalty of invention. A photographer has the opposite: the world presents itself and then it's gone. But the eye you bring to that moment is everything. And for mine, the painters built it.
