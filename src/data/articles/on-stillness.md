There is a photograph I return to often. A doorway, midday light, no one inside. It was taken in 1972 and I have never been able to find out who took it. What I know is that every time I look at it, I feel the weight of a moment that no longer exists — and that this weight grows heavier the longer I stare.

Motion cannot do that. A film frame passes before you've decided how to feel about it. The still image waits.

## The urgency of the fixed frame

We tend to think of photography as passive — a record, a document, a trace. But the longer I work with a camera, the more I believe the opposite. The still image is the most aggressive form of time we have. It doesn't approximate a moment. It arrests it. Forces it to stand trial.

Video gives you duration. Photography gives you confrontation.

The philosopher Roland Barthes called this quality the *punctum* — the detail in a photograph that pricks you, that breaks through the surface of the image and reaches something personal and undefended. You don't choose it. It chooses you. And it can only happen because the image is still. Because you have time to be found by it.

## What motion takes away

I shoot both film and stills. I understand the power of moving images — I've spent years in dark rooms watching footage and feeling something I can only describe as being carried. But being carried somewhere is not the same as being stopped in your tracks.

When everything moves, the eye negotiates constantly. It follows, anticipates, adjusts. The brain is always a little ahead of the image, preparing for the cut. In a still photograph, there is no cut coming. There is only this.

That *only this* is where photography lives.

## Time as material

The photographers I admire most — Francesca Woodman, Masahisa Fukase, Saul Leiter — understood that time itself is a material, like light or grain. A long exposure doesn't just blur motion. It layers time. It says: this moment contained all of these moments, simultaneously, and now they are one thing.

You cannot do that in video. Video moves through time. Photography holds it.

I don't think one is better than the other. But I do think that stillness carries a particular kind of urgency that we underestimate — especially now, when images move faster than we can process them.

The still photograph asks you to stop. In a world that never does, that might be the most radical thing it can offer.
